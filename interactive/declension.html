What is declension? — the linguistic analogy

Many languages mark a noun's role in a sentence by changing its form, not its meaning. The noun (its stem) stays; a case-ending or particle — the role marker — says what job it is doing. That is declension.

stem (the thing)role markerreads as
Latinros- (rose) -a / -am / -aerose as subject / object / possessor

Two more terms this work borrows: syncretism — two roles that happen to share one form (Latin -ae is both genitive and dative singular; here language and demonym both surface as “Italian”). And fusion — the ending isn't cleanly detachable from the stem; they blend phonologically.

The finding: an LLM does the same. The country is the stem (operand); the relation — currency-of, capital-of, language-of… — is the case (operator): an addable direction that factorizes from the country, with its own syncretism and fusion. Below, watch it happen.
